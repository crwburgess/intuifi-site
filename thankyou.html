Why customer insight rarely drives strategic decisions

The short version
Leaders want to be led by customer insight. Most are working from a filtered, incomplete, and quickly outdated version of it.
  • Direct customer conversations take time leaders rarely have, so insight often arrives filtered by someone else.
  • Research gets commissioned, produced, and shelved before it has a chance to drive change.
  • Internal dynamics, seniority, and conviction regularly override what customers actually said.
  • AI is being used to synthesise feedback, but the output is hard to trust without traceability.
  • As organisations grow, customer voices get replaced by internal opinion. Speaking to customers gets perceived as something that slows decisions down rather than improves them.
  • Leaders believe they are receiving an accurate picture of what customers need. The people passing it up the chain often offer a sanitised version — sometimes to protect themselves or their team from negative feedback being seen as a reflection on their performance.
  • Leaders commission research to inform decisions, but the people who produced it rarely know whether it was ever used or what changed as a result.

Key themes from the research

In the early days, founders described speaking to customers directly and knowing exactly what they needed. As their companies grew, that changed. One product leader described realising they could no longer sustain direct customer contact and run the business at the same time. They started relying on others to filter what mattered, and over time lost confidence that what was reaching them was the full picture.
Sales teams described giving leaders summaries shaped by what they thought leadership wanted to hear. Customer success teams described focusing on keeping customers happy rather than capturing strategic insight. Engineers described never speaking to customers at all.
So what?
The people between a leader and their customers are not filtering insight deliberately. They are just doing their own jobs, which means strategic signals get lost without anyone noticing.
Recommendation
Set up a regular direct line to customer-facing teams, separate from management reporting. Ask them to flag recurring themes rather than individual issues. But if you can, prioritise as much time with customers as you can afford.
One product leader described spending their innovation budget on research reports that got looked at once and put aside. Another described acting on only 15 to 20 percent of insights coming from customers — not because the rest were unimportant but because they got forgotten. One described a decision that could have been made in two weeks taking seven months because of the friction involved in getting stakeholder alignment.
A head of marketing described spending significant personal effort conducting customer interviews, writing them up, and uploading them to a shared drive. They noted it was unlikely anyone had looked at them since.
So what?
The bottleneck is not producing the research. It is that once it exists, there is no lightweight way to keep it visible. So it gets replaced by whatever conversation happened most recently.
Recommendation
When research is produced, share it widely internally with the reasoning behind it. When a decision is made, communicate the outcome and why, whether you acted on the findings or not. Store it somewhere people can refer back to. A report that justified not doing something is as valuable as one that did.
Several described situations where customer insight existed but did not change the outcome. Plans had been outlined and nobody challenged them. One product leader said directly that insight needs to be trustworthy, because otherwise you are going up against someone's ego in the room.
A consultant with experience across multiple companies described a pattern they saw repeatedly: product managers who had already decided what to build used customer research to validate the decision rather than question it. Another described situations where nobody challenged a plan because they did not want to be seen as causing problems.
So what?
When a finding cannot be traced back to a real source, it becomes a matter of opinion. And opinion tends to defer to seniority.
Recommendation
Where possible, include direct customer quotes alongside findings. A verbatim comment from a customer is harder to dismiss than a summary of what customers said.
Almost every leader was using or considering AI to help make sense of customer feedback. One described AI tools as better suited to confirming what you already believe than producing objective analysis. Another described spending three to four hours manually converting AI output into a structured report so it was easy to share with others in the leadership team.
A head of marketing described using ChatGPT heavily for analysis but noted it was done with a human sense check rather than a detailed deep dive. A product manager described wanting AI to help identify where to focus their analysis rather than do the analysis itself.
So what?
The manual work of making AI output trustworthy enough to share is not visible to the people receiving it. Leaders see a clean summary. They do not see the hours spent getting it there.
Recommendation
When time is short it is easy to share AI-generated findings without reading them through first. It is worth taking a few minutes to check them yourself before they go wider. It takes less time than dealing with the questions that come back if something turns out to be wrong.
Several described a point in their company's growth where decisions that should have been grounded in customer insight became internal exercises instead. One founder described a decision that could have been made in two weeks taking seven months — not because of a lack of insight but because the process involved passing a spreadsheet around the company for internal comments. By the time a decision was reached, the customer's voice had been replaced by a committee of internal opinions.
Sales teams described feedback as something that competes with their primary job. One said the time spent on it was time not spent selling, and without clear incentives to capture it, it did not get done. An engineer described being handed requirements with no context about why, meaning the customer need that originated the work had been lost entirely by the time it reached them.
So what?
The people responsible for capturing insight have their own priorities. Without a structure that makes it easy and expected, it does not happen consistently. And with every hand it passes through, a little more context gets lost.
Recommendation
Be clear on which customers have raised challenges and ensure they are spoken to before action is taken to validate what you are hearing directly. It removes a layer of interpretation, gives you a cleaner signal, and reduces the risk of solving the wrong problem.
